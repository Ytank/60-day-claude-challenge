DECISION REPORT

Pursue a core cybersecurity role now vs. fallback into teaching cybersecurity

July 17, 2026

The real decision

You're deciding whether to keep pushing to land a core cybersecurity role right now, or accept your current roadblocks and pivot to teaching cybersecurity to students as Plan B.

The real trade-off: a 3-month notice period and limited practical experience are slowing your job search, while teaching is more immediately available but moves you away from your CISO goal.

It's hard because your gut already picked Option A, but the obstacles are real and haven't gone away just because you're sure it will work out.

The case for each option

Option A — Land a core cybersecurity role now

Strongest case: It's the direct path toward your long-term goal of becoming a CISO at a major bank in Dubai or Switzerland. Every year in a core role compounds toward that.

Hidden upside: Even a modest entry role gives you the practical experience you say you're currently lacking — the very thing blocking you.

Biggest weakness: The 3-month notice period and thin hands-on experience make you a harder sell against candidates who can start sooner or show more field work.

Best if you value: long-term career trajectory over short-term ease.

Option B — Teach cybersecurity to students

Strongest case: It's accessible now and keeps you inside the cybersecurity field while you figure out the job search.

Hidden upside: Teaching forces you to deepen and organize your own technical knowledge — which could directly patch your "lack of practical knowledge" gap.

Biggest weakness: It doesn't build the operational, hands-on security experience that hiring managers — or a future CISO track — actually require.

Best if you value: stability and staying engaged in the field over speed toward the CISO goal.

Assumption buster

Assumptions you may be making:

1. That "it will work out" is a plan rather than a hope — you haven't named what specifically resolves the notice period or experience gap.

2. That teaching is purely a fallback, when it could also be reframed as leverage (visibility, credibility) rather than retreat.

3. That a good company will overlook a 3-month notice period without you needing to change how you're positioning yourself.

Biases at play: Optimism bias (assuming the outcome without a concrete plan) and status quo bias (staying in your current role/track because it's familiar, despite the roadblocks).

What you're definitely ignoring: The practical-knowledge gap won't close itself just by waiting for an offer — it needs an active plan regardless of which option you pick.

Premortem

Option A failed after 12 months, because:

1. The 3-month notice period kept losing you roles to faster-moving candidates. 2. You never closed the practical-knowledge gap before interviews. 3. You held out too long without adjusting your approach.

Early warning sign: Multiple interview rejections citing "hands-on experience" as the reason.

Prevention action: Start building demonstrable hands-on experience now, in parallel with applying.

Option B failed after 12 months, because:

1. Teaching pulled you further from the technical, operational work hiring managers want. 2. It quietly became permanent rather than a bridge. 3. Your CISO timeline slipped without you noticing.

Early warning sign: You stop actively applying for core roles altogether.

Prevention action: Set a fixed review date to reassess whether teaching is still "Plan B" or has become the default.

7-day test plan

Day 1-2: Research 5 companies actively hiring core security roles and note their stance on notice periods.
Day 3-4: Complete one small hands-on project (lab, CTF, or cert exercise) to start closing the practical-knowledge gap.
Day 5-6: Have one direct conversation with a recruiter or hiring manager about how they view your notice period and experience level.
Day 7: Decide: if you have at least one warm lead or clear feedback path, keep pushing Option A; if not, seriously activate Option B in parallel.

The verdict

Option A — Pursue the core cybersecurity role

It scores higher overall and is the only path directly aligned with your CISO goal. Your gut is right — but "it will work out" needs to become a concrete plan to close the experience gap.

What could flip it: If 60-90 days pass with no traction despite real effort, Option B becomes the smarter move, not a defeat.

Confidence isn't a substitute for a plan — you still have to close the experience gap yourself.
